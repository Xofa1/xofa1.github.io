After the villain Groll tries to plunge the peaceful village of Riverside into chaos, it's up to the protagonist Ruffy to try and stop him. His adventure will take him to many fantastic places, where he will have to fight bad guys, solve puzzles, and make new friends along the way. Luckily for Ruffy, he can use his magical swap ability to overcome the many challenges he faces along the way. Need a way up a mountain? Swap the waterfall for a wall of vines! Need a path across the water? Swap the stone pillars for wooden ones that float on water!

Ruffy and the Riverside brings back the vibes of old-school adventure games with unique gameplay mechanics and a colorful, hand-drawn game world for a story-driven and fun experience.

Own Experience

Indie Development

As part of a six-month long internship, I had the opportunity to join the in Nuremberg-based indie studio Zocklabs and participate in the development of their original game Ruffy and the Riverside. This was my first game project outside of personal and university projects, so it allowed me to gain valuable insight into the game industry through the lens of an indie studio.

Sound Design

A big part of my work on Ruffy and the Riverside was sound design. I was pretty much given free rein in how the soundscapes could be designed. To get an idea of what I wanted the game to sound like, I first familiarized myself with the existing parts of the game. Looking at the different levels, even playing some of them, reading the storyboard and the level design document allowed me to get a feel for the game. Then I started thinking about what sounds the player should be able to hear in the game world, and what sounds could enhance the player's experience. These sounds basically got compiled in to a list, which later got extended in to a fully fledged sound design document, giving overview of the planned and implemented sounds per game level. In this creative process of filling the game world with imaginary sounds, I was inspired by watching and listening to similar games in the genre that are known for their great sound design, such as The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild. My idea of the overall sound design for the game concluded to be a mix of realistic sounds to make the world feel alive and immersive, as well as more cartoonish and fun sounds that fit the art style and casual vibe of the game.

After I've gotten an idea on how the game should sound, I started looking for fitting and useable sounds in sound libraries to create the sounds that I needed. Trough methods such as cutting, equalization, layering, looping, changing pitch and panning, I edited the "raw" sounds in to the finished sounds, the player hears when playing the game. For this process of creating sounds for Ruffy and the Riverside, I used the Reaper digital audio workstation. After prototyping the sounds and implementing them into the game I usually presented them to my colleagues and gathered feedback and approval.

Audio Implementation

My work on Ruffy and the Riverside also included implementing sound into the game engine. The audio implementation was done directly through the Unity engine, without any additional audio middleware. Depending on the complexity of the application area, sounds were played directly through Unity's built-in AudioSource component, through a UnityEvent or through a custom script.
